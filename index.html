Welcome to Columbia University from Spectator!

First-years,

Welcome to Columbia—and get ready for one of the most tiring, exhilarating, and important weeks of your four years here. Before you jump fully in to our Orientation Guide, we want to tell you a little bit about Spectator, how we can help you make the most out of NSOP, and how you can get involved.

Get the most out of NSOP

NSOP is a time to be uncomfortable, meet new people, and learn how to live and thrive on campus. Life at Columbia can be frustrating and stressful, but also truly amazing if you know how to play your cards right.

That’s where we come in. We’re your eyes and ears around campus, creating content across a variety of platforms that informs, entertains, and allows you to get the most out of your Columbia experience.

We’ve put a lot of work into helping you through NSOP. This Orientation Guide will help you with everything you need to do to get acclimated. You’ll learn where to eat, drink, go, and study both on and off campus. You’ll learn how to register for classes, where to find key resources around campus, and figure out which clubs might suit you. We’ve also compiled a list of the top stories we think you need to follow this year and the 116 Columbia traditions that will make you a true Columbian.

After checking out this site, you should sign up for our NSOP Wake Up Call Newsletter, a daily newsletter that will break down which orientation events you won’t want to skip and bring you live NSOP coverage and helpful guides to Columbia life from Spectrum, our 24/7 blog.

About Spectator

Spectator is a financially independent student-produced collection of publications, products, and events. We create content that informs, engages, and entertains the community with a goal of enhancing the Columbia experience.

At the core of what we do is the Columbia Daily Spectator, the nation’s second oldest college daily that has maintained a strong tradition of using a skeptical eye to accurately report the news over its 139 year history.

You can learn more about each of our publications and products by clicking the icons above.

Joining Spectator

We believe joining our staff is the best way to spend your extracurricular time at Columbia. Working at Spectator makes you grow as a person, thinker, and a leader in a way that empowers you in any career you pursue. Through doing the work of journalism, business, tech, and more, we learn how to run a real company—with real consequences. The skills students learn at Spectator can prove valuable in a variety of fields, and we’ve had alumni go off and have successful careers in journalism, consulting, banking, politics, entertainment, medicine, law, and more.

Spectator also offers the best work-study jobs on campus. If you qualify for work-study and are accepted in a staff position, you are eligible to apply into our work-study program right away.

Come to an open house or check our website (specpublishing.com/join) to learn more!

We hope you find this site useful, and feel free to reach out to us directly with any questions or comments at editor@columbiaspectator.com

  • Import your desired courses into your Wish List during registration via SSOL.

Ways to Save Money and Eat Cheap

Eat@CU Rewards

If you're going to always spend your Friday happy hour at Bernheim & Schwartz, you might as well as get rewarded for it. Eat@CU, an app developed by Spectator—the parent company of the Columbia Daily Spectator—has partnerships with a number of restaurants in the neighborhood, including the Heights, 16 Handles, Thai Market, Mel's Burger Bar, and Bernheim. When you dine at any of these restaurants, you'll get a star on your phone — which you can use for free food and drinks down the line.

Eat@CU Deals

You can also reap the benefits of discounts and deals at the restaurant and bars in the area. It's like a virtual happy hour or special menu. Some current deals you can enjoy through the app include "$19.95 unlimited tacos and sangria at Amigo's," "an all-night happy hour at Bistro Ten 18," and "10 percent off 16 Handles."

CU Meal Share

Students are finding ways to help save money on meals. The First-Generation Low-Income Partnership started CU Meal Share. Students who want to share meal swipes will post the dining hall location, date and time, descriptions of themselves (so you can identify them), and contact information on the group page. To accept a meal swipe, simply text/message/contact the person who posted on the page.

Graduate!

  • Columbia and Barnard have a contractual relationship allowing for students from both schools to live, eat, and take classes on either side of the street. Barnard has its own budget, president, and board of trustees, but Columbia confers Barnard’s degrees. For many students, though, the distinctions between the two schools are murky at best

  • Tom’s Restaurant, located on the corner of Broadway and 112th Street, is famous for being used as the exterior of Monk's Cafe in “Seinfeld.”

  • Samad’s Gourmet is owned by the same family that owns The Heights, a favorite watering hole in Morningside Heights.

  • Just north of Morningside Heights, Manhattanville is a former industrial neighborhood that will be home to a new Columbia campus. The campus, currently under construction, will provide the University with 17 acres of badly needed space, but has been highly controversial among locals.

  • Vampire Weekend consists of four Columbia graduates, including lead singer and guitarist Ezra Koenig, CC ’06, pictured here at the 2014 Governor's Ball Music Festival. Vampire Weekend also performed at Columbia's 2009 Bacchanal.

  • Koronet is the perfect late-night stop thanks to its cheesy pizzas that come in gigantic sizes—hello, 32-inch pies!

  • Tom’s Restaurant, located on the corner of Broadway and 112th Street, is famous for being used as the exterior of Monk's Cafe in “Seinfeld.”

  • Columbia has 11 fraternities, and five sororities.

  • During the 2013-15 school years, student activism has centered around Columbia’s sexual assault policy and the University’s investment in private prison companies. Student protests proved fruitful this past summer when Columbia became the first U.S. university to divest from the private prison industry.

  • In the spring of 2014, the controversial decision by Barnard’s administration to remove a Students for Justice in Palestine banner led to a series of protests and counterprotests by pro-Israeli and pro-Palestinian student groups. These dialogues flare up again every spring when students erect a wall on Low Plaza during Israeli Apartheid Week to protest what they believe is Israel’s apartheid wall.

  • Barack Obama, Nelson Mandela, and Bill Clinton have all dined at Sylvia’s.

  • Medical Services is housed on the third floor of John Jay Hall.

  • Glass House Rocks is an annual student-run event where campus groups showcase their work in Lerner Hall. This is one of the few times a year when many groups come together to put on amazing performances for students.

  • Orchesis is a popular dance group that puts on a big show each semester.

  • Every semester, students come together the night before finals begin to unwind and participate in Midnight Breakfast, which is a free breakfast hosted in Barnard’s LeFrak Gym.

  • President Bollinger kicks off the academic year every fall with the Fun Run, a 5 kilometer run/walk through Riverside Park that’s open to all Columbia students, faculty and staff.

  • In the 2014-15 school year, Columbia’s men’s and women’s fencing teams came home with an Ivy League title, an NCAA Championship, and No. 1 rankings for both squads. If their tournaments are too far away—they recently went to South Korea for the World University Games—check out sports like basketball and volleyball that play on campus.

  • The front of Butler’s frieze boasts these names: Homer, Herodotus, Sophocles, Plato, Aristotle, Demosthenes, Cicero, and Vergil.

  • Cannon’s is actually now called Tara Hill. But we still call it Cannon’s.

  • The stained glass windows, the flying buttresses, and the beautiful view from the roof will make the vertical tour of Saint John the Divine the best part of Art Hum, as long as you’re not afraid of heights.

  • 40s on 40 is an annual, unofficial celebration typically held 40 days before Commencement, during which seniors gather on Low Steps to drink and reminisce.

  • You’ll probably emerge from Butler bleary-eyed on more than a few mornings.

  • In April 2012, Daniel Radcliffe came to campus to film scenes of “Kill Your Darlings,” a Sony Pictures Classics movie about Columbia's famed Beat writers. Radcliffe, pictured here on Low Plaza in costume, portrayed Allen Ginsberg in the film. More recently, in March 2014, Julianne Moore was spotted jogging along College Walk for the film “Still Alice,” for which she won an Academy Award for Best Actress.

  • Mudd is known as one of the easier roofs to climb on. It’s against the rules, of course.

  • First Lady Michelle Obama and her daughters were spotted dining at Morningside Brunch favorite Community in the spring

  • The halal cart on 114th is a favorite for late night grease among Columbia students.

  • If Butler is filled to capacity, try to find a seat in NoCo or Avery.

  • Orgo Night is a semesterly event held by the Columbia University Marching Band in Butler 209 at midnight just before the Lit Hum final. The band tells jokes about campus happenings and plays songs along with its performance.

  • Started at Barnard in 1978, Take Back the Night aims to create a safe space for sexual assault survivors and promote discussion about sexual violence on college campuses.

  • Postcrypt is a student-run music venue that hosts folk concerts every Friday and Saturday night in St. Paul’s Chapel’s basement. It also runs a coffeehouse that serves baked goods and sweet drinks.

  • The World Leaders Forum is an annual series of events held in Low Rotunda, where global thought leaders and heads of state, like His Excellency Dr. Mohammad Ashraf Ghani, president of the Islamic Republic of Afghanistan, are invited to speak to and engage with students.

  • Whenever a big snowstorm hits, Facebook events for snowball fights on South Lawn are bound to appear. During one such snowball fight in January 2014, a CNN reporter was caught in the crossfire on live television.

  • The lawns are notoriously closed most of the time—but Dean of Undergraduate Student Life Cristen Kromm said she’s trying to change that for the upcoming semester.

  • The Low Steps are a popular study and hangout spot in the spring and early fall.

  • Columbia University Emergency Medical Service, previously known as CAVA, is a student-operated, New York state-certified basic-life volunteer ambulance corp. It responds to calls made all throughout campus 24 hours a day, seven days a week.

  • Butler’s roof is a bit trickier to climb on than Mudd and IAB.

  • During Bacchanal 2013, Katie Furr (aka Fountain Girl), CC ’14, rose to fame by climbing into the upper part of the Low Plaza fountain and splashing water on the crowd. Public Safety waited patiently for her to finish before taking her away.

  • Your CUID gives you free admission into over 30 museums around the city. If you can’t get into a museum for free, fret not: Most museums have Pay-What-You-Wish hours, which means you’ll still be able to get into the new Whitney Museum in Chelsea for a relatively low cost.

  • Riverside Park boasts a number of notable monuments, including Grant’s Tomb at W 122nd.

  • Jeffrey Sachs is, among many other things, a world-famous economist, director of Columbia’s Earth Institute, and special adviser to United Nations Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon.

  • The Westside salad bar is a favorite among students.

  • It’s a 9.5 mile walk.

  • Oren’s owner is the brother of the owner of brewery Bernheim & Schwartz.

  • Housing policy requires that any student without a campus housing assignment must be signed in by residents (through a new policy change is supposed to ease weekend congestion). Due to this strict policy, the East Campus lobby is often crowded and noisy on weekend nights.

  • When Homecoming rolls around in October, show some school spirit and take the free shuttle or the 1 train up to Baker Athletics Complex, at Broadway and 218th Street. Don't know much about sports? That's OK. Neither do the people in this video.

  • Hillel hosts Friday night Shabbat dinner each week.

  • Every winter, College Walk is illuminated by the glow of lights hung on the campus’s trees. At the end of November or the beginning of December, students come together for the festive Tree Lighting and Yule Log ceremonies.

  • 1020 is known for being one of graduate students’ favorite bars.

  • Barnard has eight research librarians.

  • Check out Citi Field, Yankee Stadium, and Barclays Center. Buy tickets to a game—if you must.

  • Yes, you have to pass a swim test to graduate from Columbia.

  • Or, get a room.

  • Every May, Low Plaza and South Lawn fill up with light blue robes and eager friends and family for the University Commencement ceremony.

What happened last year: Spectator took a look at undergrad courses last semester and found that over 65 percent of classes in Columbia’s 10 most popular majors failed to list textbook information. What to look out for this semester: Spectator will take another look into textbook price information to see if University efforts have been effective, and if professors have been following the law.

Diana Center

Liz’s Place: Grab a coffee and snack at the coffee shop inside the first floor of the Diana Center, and get some work done on the comfy red seats. It can get very crowded, especially during lunchtimes and late afternoons when people want coffee, so try to get there earlier if you want a seat. Don’t work here if you need a quiet place, since there will be loud music, coffee machine sounds, and student chatter throughout the day. They also take Barnard points and Flex!
  • Pros: You’re in the perfect spot if you need caffeine and food. The radio usually plays top 10 music if you like that, the big red seats are comfortable, and there are Mac computers that you can use.
  • Cons: It will get very crowded with long lines during prime caffeinating times (morning, lunchtime, late afternoon). The tables there are super low, which aren’t conducive to working.
  • Tips: You get a discount on drinks if you bring a reusable mug
  • Hours: Monday through Thursday: 8 a.m. to 2 a.m.; Friday: 8 a.m. to 8 p.m.; Saturday: closed; Sunday: 4 p.m. to 11 p.m.
Second Floor McIntosh Dining Room: Take advantage of lots of seating when it’s not breakfast and lunch on the second floor of the Diana.
  • Pros: Lots of tables and seats at which you can spread out, relatively quiet when the café isn’t open, close proximity to Liz’s Place if you want to grab coffee and a snack, great natural light
  • Cons: Only works as a study space when the café is closed since students will be using the dining room to eat; tables can sometimes be dirty if you get there right after lunch
  • Tips: Get there around late afternoon or early evening. There’s also seating outside of the dining room near the staircase, but it will be louder there.

Under the Magnolia Tree

Take advantage of the last few weeks of nice weather, and park yourself under the magnolia tree for some relaxing study time.
  • Pros: It’s the perfect outdoor reading spot, and it’s situated right in the middle of Barnard’s campus.
  • Cons: Working outdoors can be distracting, especially in a high traffic area like the under of the magnolia tree.
  • Tips: The magnolia tree will permanently move to the Diana Center terrace in November, so make sure you get plenty of photos with the tree in its original location.

NoCo

Science & Engineering Library: Located inside the Northwest Corner Building, the Science & Engineering Library is the quintessential SEAS student library. (Of course, anyone can study here, though.)
  • Pros: Great natural lighting on all floors, individualized study spaces, and working printers on both floors that are almost never broken.
  • Cons: Can get very crowded quickly since it’s a popular study spot, computers are slow, farther from first-year dorms.
  • Tips: First floor is not designated to be a quiet study space, so head to the second floor if you’re looking for a quiet experience.
  • Hours: Monday through Thursday: 9 a.m. to 11 p.m.; Friday: 9 a.m. to 1 a.m. Saturday: 11 a.m. to 11 p.m.; Sunday: 11 a.m. to 10 p.m.
Joe Coffee: Joe Coffee is another great place to grab a cappuccino and snack to work—if you can snag a coveted seat. The place fills up fast, but it has a great view and nice ambiance.
  • Pros: Entirely glass walls, so there’s plenty of natural light, good coffee, and pastries.
  • Cons: It’s rare to find a free table when you arrive, and the coffee is on the pricey side.
  • Tips: Even if a table is taken, people are usually willing to share if the table has a free seat—don’t be afraid to ask! If you head upstairs, there are also a few benches and seats that you can use (but they’re not a part of Joe). Also, don’t forget to get a punch card when you buy your coffee. You’ll earn a free one after 10 trips.
  • Hours: Monday through Friday: 8 am. to 8 p.m.; Saturday through Sunday: 9 a.m. to 6 p.m.

Hungarian Pastry Shop

Indulge in delicious pastries and bottomless cups of coffee at this cozy spot. The shop is perfect for anyone who enjoys a café atmosphere, but it’s probably not the best place if you need to hunker down for an exam because of its dim lighting and background noise.
  • Pros: Another great café-style study spot away from campus (and right next to the Cathedral of Saint John the Divine), open relatively late, $4 for unlimited coffee refills
  • Cons: Farther from campus, no Wi-Fi (but you can connect to Columbia’s Wi-Fi if you’re lucky), space isn’t very large so it can get crowded quickly, no power outlets.
  • Tips: Don’t forget to bring cash because they don’t take credit/debit cards.
  • Hours: Monday through Friday: 7:30 a.m. to 11:30 p.m.; Saturday: 8:30 a.m. to 11:30 p.m.; Sunday: 8:30 a.m. to 10:30 p.m.
  • Address: 1030 Amsterdam Ave, New York, NY 10025 (between Cathedral Parkway and 111th Street)

Max Caffe

This homey and well-armchaired café is a bit of a trek—it’s located between 122nd Street and 123rd Street on Amsterdam Avenue—but you’ll be well-rewarded for your travels. With free Wi-Fi, tasty sandwiches, and smaller crowds compared to those of Hungarian and Joe, Max Caffe is a solid place to spend the day studying.
  • Pros: Waitstaff is very tolerant of students staying long hours, and you have the option of ordering food more substantial than coffee and pastries. Great hours as well—open to 12:30 a.m. every day if you’ve got a long study session ahead.
  • Cons: The Wi-Fi can be spotty at times. Some of the armchairs are so plush that you risk falling asleep.
  • Tips: Come during the day when the place has few patrons. If you’re over 21, study until early evening, then enjoy happy hour drinks before heading back to campus.
  • Hours: Monday through Sunday: 8 a.m. to 12:30 a.m.
  • Address: 1262 Amsterdam Ave, New York, NY 10027

Business and Economics Library

The Business and Economics Library lies within Uris Hall, home of Columbia Business School. It’s pretty unique in that it’s a library that students can talk in, which makes it good for group projects and study sessions. Plus, the spaciousness and high ceilings diffuse noise, so it’s not distracting if you’d rather study solo.
  • Pros: You can bring food into this library! It’s also got all the amenities—industrial-sized staplers, hole punchers, and paper cutters, that is.
  • Cons: You’re sharing the space with business school students, so the place can get super crowded, especially around lunch time and dinner time.
  • Tips: Unless you’re an economics major, don’t bother coming here during midterms and finals. The library actually locks out non-econ students to save room for the business students.
  • Hours: Monday through Wednesday: 8 a.m. to 12 a.m.; Thursday: 8 a.m. to 10 p.m.; Friday: 8 a.m. to 9 p.m.; Saturday: 10 a.m. to 9 p.m.; Sunday: 10 a.m. to 11 p.m.

Brownie's Cafe

To find this hidden cafe, turn in the atrium of Avery Hall, head down the stairs, and follow your nose. This delicious eatery doubles as a popular study space with long, spacious tables.
  • Pros: Plenty of table space and actually one of the more affordable and tasty places to eat on campus.
  • Cons: No power outlets, and the place can sometimes be overrun with English grad students. The café is also closed on the weekends.
  • Tips: Even though you don’t need to buy anything to be in the seating area, bring cash if you’d like to indulge. Also, even though the cafe closes at 6:30 p.m. most days, the tables remain open for use after hours.
  • Hours: Monday through Thursday: 9 a.m. to 5 p.m.; Friday: 9 a.m. to 3 p.m.

Nussbaum

Nussbaum & Wu is known more for its bagels than study space, but its tables are spacious with plenty of outlets. It’s a great place to buy yourself a coffee and hunker down over your books.
  • Pros: You’ll usually be able to find a seat.
  • Cons: The food is kind of pricey, and if you’re here for more than 30 minutes, you WILL smell like bagels. Also, no Wi-Fi.
  • Tips: Avoid mealtimes—the place can get very noisy at peak hours.
  • Hours: Monday through Sunday: 6 a.m. - 10 p.m.
  • Address: 2897 Broadway, New York, NY 1Monday through Sunday: 6 a.m. - 10 p.m.

Schapiro Lounge

Located on the ground floor of Schapiro Hall, the Schapiro lounge is a solid study space that is relatively uncrowded most of the time.
  • Pros: It’s got pretty much everything you need close at hand: a printing lab down the hall, vending machines, and plenty of seats and outlets. There’s even a halal cart right outside the building to satisfy those greasy, late-night cravings.
  • Cons: Random events are held in the lounge, so your study sessions may be interrupted by Wednesday night yoga.
  • Tips: Don’t be put off by the fact that this space is in a dorm—the place is pretty much open to all students at all hours.

LLC Sky Lounges

The LLC Sky Lounges on the top floors of Hartley and Wallach are known for being first-year haunts.
  • Pros: If you live in Hartley, Wallach, or John Jay, you’re in luck. When it’s snowing or raining outside but you don’t want to study in your room, head over to the Sky Lounge in your pajamas—it’s totally acceptable. There are differently sized tables that can be pushed around depending on whether you’re studying individually or with a group.
  • Cons: During prime study hours (weeknights and weekends), the lounge gets crowded, and it can be hard to find a seat. Occasionally, groups will study together, and it can also get a little loud if you like the quiet.
  • Tips: The lounge is open all day to all who have swipe access into John Jay, Hartley, and Wallach and their guests.

Low Steps

Whenever the sun comes out, you’ll be sure to see what it feels like when 90 percent of the University is studying, eating, or sunbathing on the Steps. There are plenty of seats and ledges to share!
  • Pros: The Steps are a great place to study if Butler starts to feel stuffy, and you want to be able to chat with your friends or eat as you finish your Lit Hum reading.
  • Cons: If you’re easily distracted or love people watching, you might not be that productive while you’re studying. It can also get windy, so be careful of your papers blowing away!
  • Tips: Climb up to the top of the Steps to where the lions sit on either side of Low Library and use the sides of the statues for a solid surface to lean on.

Starr East Asian Library

The Starr East Asian Library in Kent is one of the hidden gems on campus in terms of study spaces.
  • Pros: Because so few people realize that there is a library in Kent, you can usually find an open seat in this cozy and warm library.
  • Cons: The power strips between every few seats can often get crowded with bulky chargers, but don’t be afraid to ask your neighbor if you can shift his or her charger to make more room for yours.
  • Tips: The library closes pretty early, so if you’re looking to study late into the night, be sure to find a late-night spot.
  • Hours: Summer hours: Monday through Friday: 9 a.m. to 7 p.m; Saturday: 12 p.m. to 7 p.m.

Hungarian Pastry Shop

(Lounge and lower floors): Butler hardly needs mentioning as a study space. You will definitely be spending more time here than you’d like in your four years at Columbia.
  • Pros: Columbia’s only library that’s open 24/7, great for really feeling like a quintessential Columbia student. Blue Java Coffee Bar on the first floor is also super convenient for maintaining constantly high levels of caffeine in your bloodstream.

  • Hours (for cafe only): Monday through Thursday: 8 a.m. to 2 a.m.; Friday: 8 a.m. to 9 p.m.; Saturday: 9 a.m. to 6 p.m.; Sunday: 9 a.m. to 2 a.m.

Lerner Hall

Home to the Package Center, Ferris, tons of campus resources, and student group meeting spaces, Lerner is always buzzing with people. If you sit on the ramp tables or in the Piano Lounge, chances are you’ll see a familiar face.
  • Pros: Great natural light and tons of seating and tables to work, whether you prefer something a little more out in the open or more quiet space. When they’re not booked (check the little slips of papers on the doors or walls next to rooms posted by University Event Management), the ramp lounges are great spaces for group work.
  • Cons: Because so many people walk in and out of Lerner every day, studying in there can be a little noisy and distracting.
  • Tips: If you’re trying to do some reading over breakfast, lunch, or dinner and you have a meal plan, grab a plate a food from Ferris and take it to the Piano Lounge or the ramp tables for some more table or chair space than in the dining hall itself.

Carleton Commons

Recently renovated, Carleton Commons is a new study space in Mudd that opened over the summer.
  • Pros: Take advantage of everything from the comfy chairs to the sturdy tables—it’s all still new! The nice open space lets in a lot of great natural light.
  • Cons: There will be a grab-and-go cafe open close by (think Blue Java in Butler), so there will be lots of movement and noise.
  • Tips: This space is new, so we’re still gathering information about how to best use it when all students are on campus, but if you happen to have a class on the north side of campus, and NoCo is all full, check out Carleton Commons.
  • Hours: TBA

Paris Baguette

A bit out of the Columbia bubble, Paris Baguette on West 96th Street is a new-age, French-inspired bakery that just opened earlier this year.
  • Pros: What’s better than studying with sweet pastries and coffee at hand? If you know you have a long day of studying ahead, you can be sure you also have the perfect pick-me-up that will keep you going.
  • Cons: No Wi-Fi, and the food can get a little pricey, especially for some of their fancier pastries and baked goods.
  • Tips: Bring a writing assignment or history reading, and get some work done without the distraction of Facebook. If you have a cellphone plan with unlimited Wi-Fi, you can use your mobile hot spot to connect to the Internet on your laptop. If you don’t have unlimited Wi-Fi though, beware—using your hot spot on a laptop eats up a LOT of data.
  • Hours: Monday through Sunday: 7 a.m. to 10 p.m.
  • Address: 2568 Broadway, New York, NY 10025
